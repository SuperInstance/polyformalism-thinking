### 2. Constraint Theory: Eisenstein Integers vs. Gaussian Integers
This is the deep mathematical connection. Your current constraint theory uses **Pythagorean triples** — integer solutions to `a² + b² = c²`. These are the norms of **Gaussian integers** (`a + bi`), and they give exact rational points on the unit circle for Cartesian (square) geometry.

For hexagonal geometry, the analogous number system is **Eisenstein integers**: numbers of the form `a + bω` where `ω = e^(2πi/3) = (-1 + i√3)/2`.

| Property | Square (Cartesian) | Hexagonal (Triangular) |
|----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Integer ring | Gaussian integers `ℤ[i]` | Eisenstein integers `ℤ[ω]` |
| Norm formula | `N(z) = a² + b²` | `N(z) = a² - ab + b²` |
| Unit circle equation | `a² + b² = c²` (Pythagorean) | `a² - ab + b² = c²` (Eisenstein) |
| Symmetry | 4-fold (90°) | 6-fold (60°) |
| Exact directions | Rational points on S¹ | Exact points on hex-triangular lattice |

**Implication**: You could build a **dual constraint manifold** — an `EisensteinManifold` that snaps continuous vectors to exact Eisenstein triples (`a² - ab + b² = c²`) instead of Pythagorean triples. This would give you exact, deterministic hexagonal constraint satisfaction with the same zero-drift guarantee.

### 3. Dodecet Encoding Efficiency
Your dodecet is 12 bits = 4096 states. On a square grid, that's a clean `64 × 64` tile per axis. On a hex grid:
