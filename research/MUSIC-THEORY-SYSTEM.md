## 2. The Pitch Domain: Eisenstein Integers → Just Intonation

### 2.1 The Ring $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$

Eisenstein integers are numbers of the form $a + b\omega$ where $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $\omega = -\frac{1}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i$. The norm is:

$$N(a + b\omega) = a^2 - ab + b^2$$

This norm generates a hexagonal lattice in the complex plane. Every point has six nearest neighbors at distance 1 — the same sixfold symmetry as the Tonnetz.

### 2.2 The Just Intonation Lattice

In just intonation, pitches are related by frequency ratios of small integers. The fundamental ratios are:

| Ratio | Name | Harmonic |
|-------|------|----------|
| 1:1 | Unison | 1st harmonic |
| 2:1 | Octave | 2nd harmonic |
| 3:2 | Perfect fifth | 3rd harmonic |
| 4:3 | Perfect fourth | 4th/3rd harmonic |
| 5:4 | Major third | 5th harmonic |
| 6:5 | Minor third | 6th/5th harmonic |

These ratios form a lattice. Move one step along the "fifth" axis: multiply by 3/2. Move along the "third" axis: multiply by 5/4. This **is** a 2D lattice in log-frequency space, and its geometry is hexagonal.

### 2.3 The Eisenstein-Tonnetz Isomorphism

The Tonnetz (neo-Riemannian pitch lattice) places pitch classes at vertices of a hexagonal tiling. Horizontal axis = fifths (3:2). Diagonal axis = major/minor thirds (5:4 and 6:5).

Now consider log-frequency space. Let $x = \log_2(f/f_0)$ (octave-normalized frequency) and embed ratios as points in a 2D plane spanned by $\log_2(3/2)$ and $\log_2(5/4)$.

The Eisenstein lattice generated by $1$ and $\omega$ has basis vectors at 60° — exactly the angle between the fifth and third axes in the Tonnetz. The norms correspond:

- $N(1) = 1$ → unison (1:1)
- $N(1 - \omega) = 1 - 1\cdot(-1) + 1 = 3$ → perfect fifth (3:2), norm 3
- $N(2) = 4$ → octave (2:1), norm 4
- $N(2 - \omega) = 4 - 2\cdot(-1) + 1 = 7$ → ratio 7:4 (harmonic seventh)
- $N(3 - \omega) = 9 - 3\cdot(-1) + 1 = 13$ → ratio 13:8

Not every just ratio maps to an Eisenstein integer — but the ones that don't (like 5:4) map to points that lie on or near the lattice, with small deviation. The "snapping" of ratios to Eisenstein points is exactly the phenomenon of temperament: you round to the nearest lattice point, and the rounding error is the comma.

### 2.4 Harry Partch's 43-Tone Scale

Partch's 43-tone just intonation scale uses ratios up to the 11-limit (primes 2, 3, 5, 7, 11). In Eisenstein terms, this extends the lattice from 5-limit to include 7-limit and 11-limit points. The 43-tone scale is a selection of Eisenstein lattice points within one octave, chosen for maximum consonance.

The constraint-theory interpretation: each ratio is a constraint on the frequency relationship between two tones. Partch's scale is a maximal set of constraints that can be simultaneously satisfied in one octave.

## 3. Galois Connections → Voice Leading

### 3.1 The Formalism

A Galois connection between posets $(A, \leq)$ and $(B, \leq)$ is a pair of monotone maps $\alpha: A \to B$ and $\beta: B \to A$ such that:

$$\alpha(a) \leq b \iff a \leq \beta(b)$$

### 3.2 The Musical Interpretation

Let $A$ = the poset of chords (ordered by containment). Let $B$ = the poset of voice movements (ordered by constraint satisfaction — a movement $m_1 \leq m_2$ if $m_1$ satisfies all constraints that $m_2$ does).

- **$\alpha$ (chord → voices):** Decompose a chord transition into individual voice movements. Each voice gets a constraint: "move to the nearest note in the target chord."
- **$\beta$ (voices → chord):** Combine individual voice movements back into the resulting harmony.

**Unit condition:** For each voice in chord $C$, the movement prescribed by $\alpha(C)$ must land on a note that belongs to $\beta(\alpha(C))$, and $\beta(\alpha(C))$ must be contained in $C$'s allowed voicings.

**Counit condition:** The chord reconstructed from voice movements must be a valid revoicing of the original target.

### 3.3 Smooth Voice Leading

Smooth voice leading minimizes the total distance each voice travels. In the Galois framework, this is the condition that $\alpha$ produces the *tightest* possible voice assignment — the adjoint that minimizes the sum of interval distances.

A parallel fifth, for example, occurs when $\alpha$ assigns two voices to move in the same direction by the same interval. In strict counterpoint, this violates the counit condition because the resulting chord doesn't maintain independence of voices — the voices collapse onto each other in the constraint ordering.

A tritone substitution (replacing V with bII7) is a case where $\alpha$ assigns different voice movements than expected, but $\beta$ still produces a valid dominant-function chord. The Galois connection still holds — it's just a different adjunction.

## 4. Holonomy → Tonal Closure

### 4.1 Holonomy in Constraint Theory

In a fiber bundle with connection $\nabla$, the holonomy of a loop $\gamma$ measures how much a vector is rotated after parallel transport around $\gamma$. Zero holonomy = the vector returns to its starting orientation.

### 4.2 Tonal Holonomy

A chord progression is a path through the Tonnetz (harmonic space). The "parallel transport" is the transposition of the tonic reference as we move through chords.

- **Perfect cadence (V → I):** Zero holonomy. The tonic is perfectly restored.
- **Deceptive cadence (V → vi):** Nonzero holonomy. The expected resolution is thwarted. The "surprise" is exactly the holonomy class — how far from the tonic we landed.
- **Circle of fifths (C → G → D → A → E → B → F# → ... → C):** The circle closes in equal temperament (12-TET) but NOT in just intonation. The Pythagorean comma (≈ 23.5 cents) is the holonomy of the full circle.

### 4.3 The Holonomy Classification

| Holonomy Class | Musical Meaning |
|----------------|-----------------|
| 0 | Perfect resolution to tonic |
| ~0 (small) | Plagal cadence, gentle return |
| Medium | Deceptive cadence, modal mixture |
| Large | Chromatic modulation, distant key |
| Pythagorean comma | Full circle of fifths |

### 4.4 Blues Progression

The 12-bar blues: I → I → I → I → IV → IV → I → I → V → IV → I → V

In holonomy terms, the V → IV → I return has nonzero holonomy — the V → IV move goes "backward" on the circle of fifths, creating a specific holonomy signature that is the harmonic DNA of the blues. This is why the blues feels unresolved even when it returns to I — the holonomy isn't zero, it's a specific nonzero class that we've learned to hear as "home."

## 5. Negative Knowledge → Rests, Blue Notes, and Silences

### 5.1 The Constraint of Absence

In constraint theory, "negative knowledge" is what you know you DON'T know — the regions of configuration space that are explicitly excluded. In music:

- **Rests** are negative constraints: no note is played for a duration.
- **Blue notes** are violations of the scale constraint — notes from outside the diatonic set that create expressive tension.
- **Silence (fermata, breaks)** is the strongest negative constraint — all voices cease.

### 5.2 The Drama of Constraint Violation

Music is a dialectic between consonance (constraints satisfied, holonomy = 0) and dissonance (constraints violated, holonomy ≠ 0). The emotional arc of a piece is a trajectory through constraint-satisfaction space:

1. Establish constraints (tonic, key, meter)
2. Violate constraints (modulation, dissonance, syncopation)
3. Re-establish constraints (resolution, return to tonic)
4. The holonomy of the full journey = the emotional resolution

A blue note works BECAUSE it violates the diatonic constraint. The listener's brain predicts a scale tone, hears something else, and the prediction error IS the emotional content.

## 6. The Time Domain: Temporal Snap → Rhythm and Groove

### 6.1 The Snap Formalism

In Polyformalism, temporal snap maps continuous time $t$ to discrete tick indices:

$$\text{snap}(t) = \arg\min_k |t - k \cdot \Delta|$$

where $\Delta$ is the tick period. The snap gap $\delta(t) = t - \text{snap}(t) \cdot \Delta$ is the residual — what's lost in quantization.

### 6.2 Score Time ↔ Performance Time

- **$\alpha$ (score → performance):** The written rhythm is snapped to performed time with microtiming deviations. A swung eighth note isn't 50/50 — it's 67/33 or similar.
- **$\beta$ (performance → score):** Transcription — take the actual performed timing and snap it back to the nearest notation.

The snap gap $\delta$ IS the groove. In straight time, $\delta \approx 0$ everywhere. In swing, $\delta$ is systematically positive on downbeats and negative on upbeats. In funk, $\delta$ is carefully placed on specific subdivisions.

### 6.3 Spline Anchors → Bar Lines and Tempo Maps

Bar lines are the knots of a temporal spline. They anchor the rhythmic structure at regular intervals. The "curve" between knots — the actual performed rhythm — is where the feel lives.

A tempo map is a spline through tempo anchors (one per section). The tempo isn't constant — it breathes:

| Section | Typical BPM | Spline Behavior |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Intro | 60-70 | Slow, accelerating |
| Verse | 90-100 | Steady with slight rubato |
| Chorus | 120-130 | Faster, driving |
| Bridge | 80-90 | Pull back, intimate |
| Outro | 70-80 | Decelerating to stop |

### 6.4 Constraint Parameters → Musical Expression

| Parameter | Musical Meaning | Typical Range |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Deadband | Rhythmic tolerance (how far off the beat is OK) | 10-50 ms |
| Delay | Rubato elasticity (tempo stretch allowance) | 0.05-0.2 |
| Counter-rudder | Anticipation (playing before the beat) | -30 to -5 ms |
| Gain | Dynamic sensitivity (volume per expression) | 0.5-1.0 |

## 7. The 9-Channel Intent Vector → Musical Expression

The intent vector from Polyformalism maps directly to musical parameters:

### C1: Urgency → Tempo and Rhythmic Drive
High urgency = faster tempo, more rhythmic density, sixteenth notes. Low urgency = slower, longer note values, more space.

### C2: Scope → Orchestration Density
High scope = full orchestra, many voices, thick texture. Low scope = solo instrument, thin texture, exposed lines.

### C3: Confidence → Intonation and Vibrato
High confidence = centered intonation, controlled vibrato. Low confidence = pitch uncertainty, wider vibrato, microtonal wavering.

### C4: Priority → Voice Leading Hierarchy
Which voice is primary? Melody (C4 high on top voice) vs. basso continuo (C4 high on bass). Determines which voice gets the smoothest leading.

### C5: Context → Genre and Style
Jazz: extended chords, swing, blue notes. Classical: functional harmony, straight time. Blues: I-IV-V, shuffle, microtonal bends.

### C6: Intent → Emotional Trajectory
The emotional arc: tension → release, darkness → light, conflict → resolution. This is the "story" parameter.

### C7: Risk → Dissonance Tolerance
High risk = free jazz, atonality, clusters. Low risk = consonant triads, simple progressions. Controls the holonomy threshold.

### C8: Domain → Tonal Center
The key and mode. C major, D minor, F# mixolydian. The "domain" of the harmonic constraint space.

### C9: Delta → Rate of Harmonic Change
Fast delta = quickly changing chords (bebop). Slow delta = sustained harmonies (ambient, drone). The temporal derivative of the harmonic state.

### Layer 3: Composition (Slow Timescale — seconds to minutes)

The macro level: key, mode, form, phrase structure. The intent vector is set here — what emotional story are we telling?

- Key selection determines the Eisenstein lattice region we're working in
- Mode (major, minor, etc.) selects a subset of lattice points
- Form (sonata, AABA, blues, through-composed) determines the large-scale holonomy path
- Phrase structure defines the spline anchor points for the tempo map

### Layer 2: Arrangement (Medium Timescale — beats to measures)

The harmonic level: chord progression, voice leading, orchestration.

- Chord progressions are paths through the Eisenstein-Tonnetz
- Voice leading is governed by the Galois connection (smooth = adjoint)
- Orchestration maps to scope (C2) — how many voices, which instruments
- Modulations are changes of lattice region (re-anchoring the Eisenstein basis)

### Layer 1: Performance (Fast Timescale — milliseconds to beats)

The execution level: timing, dynamics, articulation, timbre.

- Temporal snap with microtiming = groove, swing, feel
- Dynamics = gain parameter applied to expression
- Articulation = envelope shape (staccato = short gate, legato = overlapping)
- Timbre = harmonic content (overtones, formants)

### Inter-Layer Communication

The layers communicate through the intent vector and constraint parameters:

```
Layer 3 sets: Key (C8), Emotional arc (C6), Form constraints
    ↓

    ↓
```

## 9. The Eisenstein Norm Correspondence Table

For each just intonation ratio, we can ask: does it correspond to an Eisenstein integer? If so, which one?

The mapping works as follows: for a ratio $n:d$, express $\log_2(n/d)$ in the basis $\{\log_2(3/2), \log_2(5/4)\}$ (fifths and thirds). The coordinates $(a, b)$ in this basis give the Eisenstein point $a + b\omega$, with norm $a^2 - ab + b^2$.

| Ratio | Name | Fifths | Thirds | Eisenstein Point | Norm | Snaps? |
|-------|------|--------|--------|-----------------|------|--------|
| 1:1 | Unison | 0 | 0 | (0, 0) | 0 | ✓ |
| 2:1 | Octave | 0 | 0* | (0, 0)+octave | 0 | ✓ |
| 3:2 | P5 | 1 | 0 | (1, 0) | 1 | ✓ |
| 4:3 | P4 | -1 | 0 | (-1, 0) | 1 | ✓ |
| 5:4 | M3 | 0 | 1 | (0, 1) | 1 | ✓ |
| 6:5 | m3 | 1 | -1 | (1, -1) | 3 | ✓ |
| 5:3 | M6 | -1 | 1 | (-1, 1) | 3 | ✓ |
| 9:8 | M2 | 2 | 0 | (2, 0) | 4 | ✓ |
| 15:8 | M7 | -1 | 1+octave | (-1, 1) | 3 | ~ |
| 9:5 | m7 | 2 | -1 | (2, -1) | 7 | ✓ |
| 16:15 | m2 | -2 | 1 | (-2, 1) | 7 | ✓ |

| 7:4 | h7 | — | — | Non-5-limit | — | ✗ |

The ratios that "snap" to Eisenstein integers are the 5-limit just intonation ratios — exactly the ones generated by primes 2, 3, and 5. Ratios involving prime 7 or 11 don't live on the standard Tonnetz; they require lattice extensions.

## 10. Formal Results

### Theorem 1: Tonnetz = Eisenstein Lattice

The 5-limit Tonnetz with axes $\log_2(3/2)$ (fifths) and $\log_2(5/4)$ (major thirds) is isometric to $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ under the norm $a^2 - ab + b^2$, up to a scale factor determined by the angle between axes.

*Proof sketch:* The angle between the fifth and third axes is $\arccos\left(\frac{\log(3/2) \cdot \log(5/4)}{|\log(3/2)| \cdot |\log(5/4)|}\right)$. This angle is approximately 60° (the Eisenstein angle), because $\log_2(3/2) \approx 0.585$ and $\log_2(5/4) \approx 0.322$, and their dot product in the log-frequency plane gives the characteristic 60° separation. The hexagonal symmetry follows.

### Theorem 2: Voice Leading Adjunction

Smooth voice leading (minimizing total voice movement) is the right adjoint $\beta$ of the chord decomposition map $\alpha$ in the Galois connection between chord posets and voice-movement posets.

*Proof sketch:* Define $\alpha(C_1 \to C_2)$ as the assignment of voices in $C_1$ to nearest targets in $C_2$. Define $\beta(V)$ as the chord formed by the targets of voice movements $V$. The adjunction $\alpha(C) \leq V \iff C \leq \beta(V)$ holds because: if the voice assignment from $C$ is "less constrained" than $V$ (i.e., $V$ achieves the same or tighter movement), then $C$ is "less than" the chord formed by $V$'s targets (i.e., $V$'s targets form a chord that contains $C$'s targets).

### Theorem 3: Holonomy Classification

The holonomy of a chord progression through the Tonnetz is a complete invariant for its harmonic function class. Progressions with the same holonomy are harmonically equivalent (can substitute for each other in a functional sense).

*This is conjectural but supported by practice:* Tritone substitutions work because they have the same holonomy class as the original dominant. The tritone substitution bII7 → I has the same voice-leading holonomy as V7 → I — the third and seventh of the chord swap roles, preserving the voice-leading distances.

## 12. Extensions and Future Work

### 12.1 Microtonal Extensions
Beyond 5-limit: 7-limit and 11-limit ratios require lattice extensions. The Eisenstein framework extends naturally — you add new axes (generators) to the lattice, making it higher-dimensional.

### 12.2 Non-Western Scales
The constraint framework is agnostic to the specific ratios. Arabic maqam, Indian raga, Indonesian gamelan — each has its own constraint set (scale, rules of movement, tonic relationships). The same formalism applies; only the constraints change.

### 12.3 Real-Time Performance
Layer 1 (performance) can run in real-time with a synthesizer. The constraint parameters become real-time control signals: a performer adjusts deadband (rhythmic looseness), gain (dynamic sensitivity), and rubato (tempo elasticity) through expression controls.

### 12.4 Composition via Constraint Satisfaction
Given a set of constraints (key, mode, emotional arc, duration, difficulty level), the system can search for progressions and voicings that satisfy all constraints simultaneously. This is classical constraint satisfaction, applied to musical configuration space.

### 12.5 Connection to Polyformalism Proper
Every primitive used here — Eisenstein integers, Galois connections, holonomy, temporal snap, constraint parameters, intent vectors — is shared with Polyformalism. Music theory is not an analogy to constraint theory; it IS constraint theory applied to acoustic configuration space.
