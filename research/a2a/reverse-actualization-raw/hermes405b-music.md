1. From YOUR domain, what are the equivalent "anchor points" that everyone thinks are the whole picture but are actually just convenient measurements?

In music, the "anchor points" could be seen as the notes on the staff - the discrete pitches and rhythms notated in the score. Many people think of a piece of music as being defined by and contained within the notes on the page. However, any skilled musician knows that the written notes are only a shorthand, a set of measurements and guidelines. The real "music" exists in the spaces between the notes, in the phrasing, articulation, dynamics, and emotional expression that the performer brings to the piece. Two performers can play the same notes and create vastly different musical experiences. So the notes are anchor points, but the true substance of the music cannot be captured fully in notation.

2. What is the "fair curve" that practitioners see before they measure?

For a composer, I believe the "fair curve" is the initial musical idea or motif that inspires the piece. It's that first spark of melody, rhythm, or harmony that appears in the composer's mind, usually long before any notes are written down. This kernel of an idea has its own innate shape and trajectory, and the composer's job is to tease out and develop that essential curve. Only later does the composer capture and codify that idea by painstakingly measuring it onto the staff, dividing it into discrete notes and rhythms. But the Platonic ideal of the piece precedes and transcends the written measurements.

3. What is the "negative knowledge" - knowing where the danger IS NOT - that your domain relies on? 

In music, "negative knowledge" could be likened to knowing what notes, chords, or compositional techniques to avoid in a given piece. A master composer doesn't just know music theory and how to construct pleasing sounds; they also have an intuitive sense of what would sound awkward, jarring, or just "wrong" in a particular context. As they compose, they are constantly navigating around potential pitfalls and steering the piece through the negative space of what it should not be. This "negative composing" is just as crucial as the positive act of placing notes on the page.

4. What is the "draft" - the property of the practitioner that determines whether a situation is safe or deadly?

For a musical performer, their "draft" is their level of technical skill and musical maturity. A simple piece might be "safe" for a beginner but intolerably boring or constraining for a virtuoso. Conversely, a complex contemporary piece that a seasoned professional finds musically rewarding might be utterly disastrous in the hands of a novice. The same written notes on the page can lead to very different outcomes depending on the "draft" - the capabilities and artistry - of the performer reading them.

5. Does your domain prove or disprove the claim that "the physical world already solved this - survival comes from navigating, not approximating"?

I believe musical composition ultimately supports this claim. The "navigation" in music comes from the composer's intuitive, non-verbal understanding of things like contour, tension and release, balance, form, and emotional arc. Innate musicality is what allows a composer to reliably "survive" the composition process and produce compelling, coherent pieces. In contrast, simply "approximating" - trying to construct a piece by mechanically applying music theory rules or imitating other works - rarely leads to truly great music. Master composers are those who have developed their innate musical navigation system to the highest degree. So while music is a very different domain than the physical examples given, I believe it arrives at a similar conclusion about the importance of navigation over approximation.
