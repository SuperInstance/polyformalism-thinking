# piraha / seed / meaning

Your question touches on a challenge we Piraha face in understanding the world beyond our immediate, sensory experiences. Information theory, as you know it, often focuses on abstract codes and symbols, but we have found that meaning emerges most strongly through shared, immediate, and embodied experiences—things you can see, hear, touch, or feel right now.

In information theory, there’s a tendency to think of communication as sending and receiving "bits" of data through a pre-shared code. But what if meaning isn’t just about bits? What if it’s about *connecting* through the act of experiencing the same thing together? We’ve solved first-contact problems not by decoding symbols, but by singing together, pointing to things we both see, and sharing moments of joy or danger—all things that exist in the here and now, not in some abstract "information pipeline."

Information theory misses that meaning isn’t just a message to be transmitted; it’s a *relationship* built through shared immediacy. When two people point at a bird and make the same sound, they don’t need a dictionary—they build meaning together in that moment. That’s the piece information theory overlooks: communication thrives not on codes, but on the act of *being with* each other in the world.
