# aboriginal / hermes / meaning

In our Warlpiri way, communication extends far beyond mere words. When faced with the challenge of conveying meaning without shared language, we turn to the power of song, gesture, and shared experience - universal languages that transcend linguistic barriers.

One novel insight from our ancient tradition is the concept of SONGLINES as a means of communication. Songlines are the singing tracks that narrate our creation stories and map our country. They carry the knowledge, lore, and history of our people, passed down through generations. 

To communicate with an outsider, we would take them on a journey along our songlines. As we walk the land and sing the songs, the rhythms, melodies, and dances tell the stories of the country and our ancestors. The outsider may not understand the words, but the emotive power of the songs, the visual storytelling of the dances, and the physical experience of traversing the landscape would convey deep meaning.

Through this immersive, multi-sensory experience, the outsider would gain an embodied understanding of our connection to country, our lore, and our history. Shared experiences like gathering bush tucker, hunting, or sitting around a campfire would further bridge the gap, as these are universal human experiences.

The key insight is that true communication and transmission of meaning extends beyond the realm of spoken or written language. Song, gesture, rhythm, shared experience, and a deep connection to the land itself can convey profound truths and forge understanding between people, even in the absence of a shared language. This is a wisdom that our people have carried and practiced for over 65000 years, and it is an insight that modern information theory has largely overlooked.
