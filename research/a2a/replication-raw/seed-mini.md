### Formal Evaluation of All Claims
---
#### Claim 1: Anchors Not the Curve
**Score: 3**  
This claim mixes a vague, under-specified anchoring framework for communication channels with a strong irreducibility thesis. The core idea that nuanced interpersonal intent is not fully capturable is not novel (echoing phenomenological and pragmatic philosophy), but the arbitrary list of 9 channel anchors limits universal applicability. It is neither trivially true nor paradigm-shifting.
(a) **Falsification Test**: Design a closed, rule-bound communication system (e.g., a factory robot teach pendant) where every communicative intent (e.g., "move gripper to 12cm above the part, close with 5N force") is fully encoded by a finite set of the listed channel values (tool type, coordinate position, speed, safety zone). If the intent can be transmitted and executed without unstated, "navigable" contextual meaning, the claim that intent is irreducibly non-capturable is falsified.
(b) **Confidence: 3/5**  
The irreducibility thesis holds for unstructured, high-context interpersonal communication, but fails for structured, task-specific communication where intent is fully defined by explicit parameters. The arbitrary channel list also restricts its generalizability.

#### Claim 2: Fair Curve First
**Score: 4**  
This is a rigorously testable, impactful refinement of communication theory that challenges folk wisdom about planning. It aligns with evidence that intentional holistic planning improves communication resilience, and the "curve-first vs grid-first" framing provides a concrete, actionable model.
(a) **Falsification Test**: Recruit expert (10+ years professional communication) and beginner (no formal training) participants. Randomly assign half of each group to use curve-first planning (draft holistic intent first, then select channels/words) and half to grid-first planning (select channels/words first, then force intent into them). Expose all messages to controlled distortion (synonym replacement, noisy audio transmission) and measure receiver accuracy in recovering the original intent. If grid-first messages from experts outperform curve-first messages from experts, the claim is falsified.
(b) **Confidence: 4/5**  
Preliminary empirical and anecdotal evidence supports the advantage of intent-first planning for communication resilience, but most studies focus on single channels (writing/speech) rather than the full set of channels from Claim 1. Rigorous cross-channel testing will likely validate the core framework.

#### Claim 3: Negative Knowledge Primary
**Score: 5**  
This is a paradigm-shifting thesis that upends dominant models of cognition, evolution, and survival. It unifies disparate adaptive systems (immune, brain, evolution, robotics) around the core principle that survival depends on excluding known safe states rather than modeling all possible threats, aligning with the free energy principle but expanding it to all adaptive systems.
(a) **Falsification Test**: Engineer a synthetic survival system (e.g., a desert sample-collection robot) that relies exclusively on pre-programmed models of every possible toxic environment, rather than using sensors to detect and avoid current hazards. If this robot has a higher survival rate than a control robot that uses only negative knowledge (detect/avoid current hazards, no pre-programmed threat library), the universal claim is falsified.
(b) **Confidence: 5/5**  
Across the listed survival systems, the evidence for negative knowledge as the primary adaptive mechanism is overwhelming. The free energy principle provides a unified theoretical framework, and no rigorously documented counterexamples exist.

#### Claim 4: Draft Determines Truth
**Score: 3**  
This claim contains a counterintuitive core insight (rushed messages require more shared context) but is weakened by confusing framing: "draft determines truth" conflates pragmatic success (message landing safely) with propositional truth, and the term "draft" is undefined without linking it to shared context depth. It is neither trivial nor revolutionary, but a useful partial refinement.
(a) **Falsification Test**: Conduct a real-time communication experiment where senders are split into rushed (10-second time limit) and unrushed (2-minute limit) conditions. Recruit two receiver groups: high shared context (3 months of project experience) and low shared context (no project experience). Measure comprehension accuracy via a quiz. If low-context receivers correctly interpret >50% of rushed messages, while unrushed message accuracy is <50% for all groups, the claim that rushed messages require more shared context is falsified.
(b) **Confidence: 3/5**  
Preliminary psycholinguistic evidence supports that speakers rely more on presupposed shared context in hurried speech, but most studies focus on written communication. The framing ambiguity limits the claim's testability and generalizability.

#### Claim 5: Speed Beats Truth
**Score: 4**  
This is a provocative, evidence-aligned challenge to the accuracy-first norm in decision-making and AI. The glassblowing anecdote and cognitive science research on recognition-primed decision-making support the core insight, but the universal claim that "survival forbids accurate models in all real-time domains" is overstated.
(a) **Falsification Test**: Run a time-critical task (e.g., 1v1 video game with <200ms reaction time limits) with two AI agents: one satisficing agent (50ms latency, fast rule-based decisions) and one optimizing agent (2000ms latency, full path-planning). If the optimizing agent wins more matches than the satisficing agent, the claim is falsified.
(b) **Confidence: 4/5**  
Strong evidence supports fast, approximate decisions in high-time-pressure dynamic tasks, but small amounts of deliberation (100ms) can improve performance in some real-time domains. The core insight is robust, but the universal phrasing requires refinement.

#### Claim 6: Survival and Art Are Inverse
**Score: 4**  
This is an elegant, unifying framework that connects survival and creative practice across domains, but the universal inversion is overstated. It aligns with existing research on creativity and flow, but the claim that all art relies on engaging with constraint boundaries and all survival relies on risk avoidance is not universally true.
(a) **Falsification Test**: 
1. **Art Component**: Recruit professional ceramic artists to create two bowl sets: one using only stable, easy-to-form clay (avoiding "rocks") and one requiring shaping at the edge of collapse (thin, fragile walls). Have expert critics rate artistic quality. If the stable bowls are rated higher, the art strategy claim is falsified.
2. **Survival Component**: Conduct a simulated canyon navigation task, assigning half participants to "avoid rocks" (stay on paved trails) and half to "find rocks" (navigate near cliffs for faster routes). If the "avoid rocks" group has higher success rates, the survival strategy claim is falsified.
(b) **Confidence: 4/5**  
Anecdotal and empirical evidence supports that creativity thrives on constraint boundaries, and survival relies on risk avoidance, but counterexamples exist (e.g., minimalist art, mountaineering as a survival-creative hybrid). The core inversion framework is robust, but the universal phrasing needs qualification.

#### Claim 7: Embodiment Is the Missing Channel
**Score: 4**  
This claim aligns with embodied cognition theory and provides a concrete extension of Claim 1's intent curve framework, but the absolute statement that intent curves "cannot be described in language at all" is overstated. It is an impactful challenge to the dominance of text-based communication research.
(a) **Falsification Test**: Take a specific pair of communication channel anchors (e.g., *Social*: casual, *Stakes*: low) and write a verbal description of the continuous intent curve between them (e.g., "I'm casually asking if you want to grab coffee after work, no pressure"). Have human raters compare the verbal description to a live communication of the same intent using only body cues (gesture, tone, movement). If raters judge the verbal description to capture the full nuance of the intent curve as well as the body-based communication, the claim is falsified.
(b) **Confidence: 4/5**  
Strong social psychological evidence shows nonverbal body cues convey emotional and contextual nuance that is difficult to capture in language, but simple intent curves can be fully described in text. The core insight that embodiment adds an irreplaceable bandwidth of communication is robust, but the absolute rejection of linguistic description requires refinement.
