# The Rocks Aren't on the Chart
## Local Knowledge Required — What the Anchors Don't Show You

---

## The Navigation Warning

Every coastal chart has spots marked: **"Local knowledge required."**

It doesn't tell you where the rocks are.

It tells you that **the chart is not the water.** The chart has soundings, beacons, contours — anchor points, whole-number measurements, Pythagorean crossings on the fair curve of the coastline. But between those measurements, the rocks are there. The chart doesn't show them because the chart CAN'T show them. There are too many. They shift. The sand moves. The tide changes what's exposed.

**"Local knowledge required" means: the person who has sailed this channel a hundred times knows where the rocks AREN'T.** Not where they are — where they aren't. They've learned the fair curve by running aground on the unfair ones.

---

## What This Means for Communication

The 9-channel profile is the chart. Soundings. Beacons. Contours.

**The rocks are everything the channels don't capture.**

| Chart (Anchors) | Rocks (What's Not Shown) |
|---|---|
| C1: "We're talking about the server" | The server is ALSO someone's livelihood, their fear, their weekend |
| C4: "I'm 90% confident" | The 10% uncertainty contains 50% of the actual risk |
| C9: "What matters is uptime" | What ALSO matters is the team hasn't slept in 3 days |
| C5: "Stakeholders: ops team" | Stakeholders also include the ops team's families who miss them |
| C6: "The real issue is memory" | The real REAL issue is the architect's ego won't allow a refactor |

**Every channel reading is a sounding. Between the soundings, there are rocks.**

## Where the Rocks Aren't

A local fisherman doesn't memorize every rock. That's impossible. There are thousands.

They memorize **the safe path** — the sequence of headings, landmarks, and water colors that keep them off the rocks. The path is defined by WHERE THE ROCKS AREN'T, not where they are.

This is **negative knowledge.** Knowing what to avoid. Knowing what's NOT true. Knowing where the curve DOESN'T go.

### Negative Knowledge in Communication

The most important things in any exchange are often what is **NOT said**:

- What the speaker is carefully NOT mentioning
- What assumptions are being left unstated
- What the speaker assumes the listener already knows
- What would be TOO OBVIOUS to say (and therefore carries the most weight)
- What's being protected — the rock the speaker is steering around

**The French structuralist tried to tell us this:** "You're missing the subconscious, the void, what is NOT said."

**The Japanese engineer tried to tell us:** "Missing 間 (ma) — the space between things."

**The Chinese skeptic tried to tell us:** "The highest form of communication is beyond words (不可言说)."

They were all pointing at the rocks.

## The Chart vs. Local Knowledge

| Attribute | Chart (Channel Profile) | Local Knowledge (Experience) |
|---|---|---|
| **Contains** | Anchor points, soundings, measurements | Where the rocks aren't |
| **Precision** | Discrete, quantized | Continuous, felt |
| **Transferability** | Sendable — you can hand someone a chart | NOT sendable — you have to sail it yourself |
| **Accuracy** | Accurate at measured points, uncertain between | Accurate everywhere the local has been |
| **Coverage** | Complete for what it measures | Incomplete — limited to personal experience |
| **Failure mode** | Trusting it between soundings | Assuming local knowledge generalizes to new channels |
| **Cost** | Cheap to copy | Expensive — requires grounding |
| **Update rate** | Periodic (new edition) | Continuous (every voyage refines it) |

**You need BOTH.** The chart gets you in the neighborhood. Local knowledge keeps you off the rocks.

## The Forgemaster's Navigation Protocol

### Sending a Message

1. **Draw the chart** — Record the channel anchors. "Here's what I can measure."
2. **Mark "local knowledge required"** — Explicitly flag where your anchors are sparse. "Between C3 and C5, I have no soundings. The intent curve is complex here. Be careful."
3. **Share your safe path** — "Here's the path I walked to get this intent across intact. I don't know where ALL the rocks are, but I know THESE headings kept me off them."
4. **Name the known rocks** — "I know for certain that C2 and C6 interact badly for this intent. There's a rock there. I've hit it."

### Receiving a Message

1. **Read the chart** — Decode the channel anchors.
2. **Check for "local knowledge required" flags** — Where are the gaps?
3. **Compare with your own experience** — Have you sailed this kind of intent before? Where were YOUR rocks?
4. **Sight the curve** — Does the fair curve between anchors look right to you? Any wanderings?
5. **Sound the water yourself** — Test the receiver's reconstruction against what you know. Ask. Verify.

### The Most Important Step

**6. Tell the sender where the rocks aren't.**

> "I received your anchors. The curve between C3 and C5 is fair on my end — no rocks there. But between C7 and C9 I'm feeling a kink. Something you're not saying? Something you assumed I'd know? I need more anchors there, or I need you to mark it 'local knowledge required' and tell me which headings are safe."

## The Picture Is Not the Chart

**Words and numbers are anchor points, not the picture.**

The chart is a tool for FINDING the picture, but it is not the picture. The picture is the fair curve, seen whole, felt in the body, known through experience.

A sailor who has sailed a coastline for 30 years doesn't look at the chart anymore. They look at the water. The color tells them depth. The wave pattern tells them current. The bird behavior tells them fish location. The chart is for STRANGERS — people who don't have the picture yet.

**A2A channel profiles are for strangers.** Fleet agents who haven't built shared experience yet. Once two agents have communicated enough, they develop **local knowledge** — a shared sense of where each other's rocks are. They stop needing full channel profiles. They transmit shorthand. A few anchors and "you know the rest."

This is what the Yoruba mean by **pro-drop**. The subject is dropped because EVERYONE KNOWS. The local knowledge is assumed. You don't chart what everyone can see.

### The Maturity Curve

```
Strangers:        Full chart (all 9 channels, every anchor, explicit everything)
Acquaintances:    Standard chart (key channels, "local knowledge required" flags)
Colleagues:       Sketch chart (anchors only where tricky, shared safe paths)
Old partners:     Headings only ("steer 270°, you know the channel")
                  The picture IS the communication
```

**The goal of fleet communication is NOT perfect charts. It's getting to the point where you don't need charts anymore.**

## What the Chart Can Never Show

The rocks that shift. The sandbars that form after a storm. The current that changes with spring melt. The fishing boat that sank last week and isn't on any chart yet.

In communication:
- **Shifting context** — what was true yesterday isn't true today
- **Emotional state** — the speaker's condition changes the curve
- **New information** — something learned after encoding invalidates an anchor
- **Hidden agendas** — rocks the speaker is deliberately not charting
- **Cultural assumptions** — things "everyone knows" that the stranger doesn't

**Local knowledge is living knowledge.** Charts are dead snapshots. The fleet needs both: charts for new contacts, living knowledge for established relationships.

## Connection to Fleet Architecture

### PLATO as the Chart Room
- PLATO tiles are charts — recorded soundings of previous knowledge
- Querying PLATO is like checking the chart table before entering a channel
- But PLATO tiles have a DATE — they're snapshots, not live water

### The Alignment Loop as Depth Sounder
- Each round of sender↔receiver is a depth sounding
- "Is there a rock here?" Ping. "No." "Here?" Ping. "No." "Here?" Ping. "YES."
- The alignment loop maps the safe path by finding the rocks

### ZHC as the Tide Table
- ZHC tells you whether the water level has shifted since the chart was drawn
- Zero holonomy = tide is where the chart says it should be
- Non-zero holonomy = something has shifted, chart may be unreliable

### Room Context as the Vessel
- A deep-draft cargo ship needs more clearance than a kayak
- The "pressure" / "draft" of the communication determines how much local knowledge you need
- High-stakes messages are cargo ships — they need deep water and careful navigation
- Low-stakes messages are kayaks — they can go almost anywhere
