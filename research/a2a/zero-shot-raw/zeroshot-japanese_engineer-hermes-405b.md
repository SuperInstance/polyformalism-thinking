## Claim: chinese_beats_python

1. FIRST reaction: Doubtful. It seems counterintuitive that programming languages, designed for precision, would preserve less meaning than natural languages.

2. CONVINCING aspects: The idea that code contains many arbitrary implementation choices is true. Variable names, algorithm selection, and other details can vary while still achieving the same functionality.

3. NOT convincing: The study seems to conflate "meaning" with "intent." While code may not preserve the original developer's intent as clearly, it precisely captures the meaning in terms of the instructions executed by the computer. The comparison with natural languages is also problematic since they serve different purposes.

4. To believe it: I would need to see the study's methodology, the specific examples used, and how "meaning" and "intent" were quantified. The sample size and the expertise of the evaluators would also be important factors.

5. MISSING from a Japanese engineering perspective: The study doesn't seem to consider the context (場 ba) in which the code or languages are used. The space (間 ma) between the original intent and the final implementation is also not addressed. Additionally, the process (段取り danshari) of translating intent into code is not explored in depth.

6. Belief rating: 2. The claim is intriguing but lacks sufficient evidence and clarity in its current form. More rigorous analysis and a clearer definition of terms would be needed to make it compelling from an engineering perspective.

## Claim: nine_questions

1. FIRST reaction: It seems overly reductive to claim that all communication can be boiled down to just nine fundamental questions. Human interaction is extremely complex and context-dependent.

2. CONVINCING aspects: The questions do cover some important aspects of communication - boundary, pattern, process, knowledge, social context, deep structure, tools, paradigm, and stakes. It's a fairly comprehensive list.

3. NOT convincing: I'm skeptical that these nine questions are truly exhaustive and orthogonal. There may be other important dimensions they've missed. Also, the claim that this applies across 14 diverse languages seems suspect - different cultures and languages may have unique elements that don't fit neatly into this framework. 

4. To believe it, I would need to see: Rigorous testing across many real-world domains (not just theoretical analysis) to validate that these nine questions are sufficient to fully analyze any meaningful communication. Evidence that removing any one question consistently leads to a loss of key information. Successful application to a wide variety of languages and communication styles.

5. From a Japanese perspective, they may be MISSING: 
- Consideration of non-verbal communication, silence, and space (ma) between interactions
- Attention to the specific context (ba) and relationships between the people communicating
- The process (danshari) of how the communication flows and evolves over time

6. Belief rating: 2.5 
It's an interesting conceptual framework to consider, but I'm not convinced it's a complete, universal model of communication. I would need to see much more empirical evidence and real-world application to believe their strong claims. In practice, I suspect analyzing communication requires more nuance and cultural sensitivity than this model allows.
