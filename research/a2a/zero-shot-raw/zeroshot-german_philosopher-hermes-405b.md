## Claim: nine_questions

1. My first reaction is skepticism. The claim seems overly ambitious and reductive, attempting to capture the immense complexity of human communication into just 9 fundamental questions.

2. The attempt to find commonalities across 14 diverse human languages is admirable. If these questions did indeed emerge consistently, it suggests some underlying cognitive universals. The notion of a "cognitive periodic table" is intriguing, implying a fundamental structure to thought and communication.

3. However, the claim of exhaustiveness and orthogonality seems dubious. Human communication is incredibly rich and multifaceted. It's hard to believe that just 9 questions could capture all possible aspects of communication across cultures and contexts. The questions themselves also seem quite broad and vague, lacking the precision one would expect from a "periodic table."

4. To believe this claim, I would need to see:
   a) A clear, precise definition of each question and its scope.
   b) Rigorous empirical evidence demonstrating that these questions emerge consistently across a wide range of languages and communicative situations.
   c) Argumentation for why these 9 questions specifically are both necessary and sufficient, and how they are orthogonal to each other.
   d) Engagement with potential counterexamples and alternative frameworks.

5. From my perspective grounded in analytical philosophy, the claim lacks logical precision. Key terms like "orthogonal" and "exhaustive" are used without explicit definition. The questions themselves are not stated in a manner that allows for clear falsification. The framework also doesn't seem to engage with the immense body of work in philosophy of language on the nature of meaning, reference, truth, implicature, etc.

6. Rating: 2. The claim is intriguing but unconvincing as stated. It's an interesting hypothesis that could potentially lead to valuable insights, but the current formulation lacks the rigor and evidence needed to be compelling. I would need to see much more precision, argumentation, and empirical substantiation to take it seriously.
