## Claim: nine_questions

1. My first reaction is skepticism. Claiming to have found a universal set of fundamental questions based on studying only 14 languages seems overly ambitious and likely to be biased towards the languages and cultures represented in the sample.

2. The idea of trying to identify core cognitive patterns underlying human communication is compelling. Breaking down complex interactions into key components or questions could provide valuable insights into how we process and convey meaning.

3. However, the claim that these 9 questions are exhaustive and orthogonal seems very questionable. With the vast diversity of human languages and cultures, it's doubtful that any small set of questions could fully capture the nuances and unique elements of all possible meaningful communication. The fact that the study only looked at 14 languages is a major red flag, as it's a tiny fraction of the over 7000 languages spoken worldwide. 

4. To lend more credence to this claim, I would want to see evidence that the 9 questions hold up when tested against a much larger and more diverse set of languages, including non-Indo-European and non-Sino-Tibetan language families. The research should also demonstrate how the questions can account for tonal and context-dependent aspects of meaning-making that are central to languages like Yoruba. Additionally, the study would need to address potential cultural biases and show that the questions aren't just an artifact of the researchers' own cognitive patterns.

5. From a Yoruba/African cognitive science perspective, a major gap seems to be consideration of how tonal languages and pro-drop languages shape cognition differently. In Yoruba, pitch is a key conveyor of meaning, and the subject of a sentence is often implied rather than stated explicitly. These features could significantly impact the types of fundamental questions that arise in communication. More broadly, the research may be overlooking cognitive patterns that are more prominent in African or other non-Western languages and cultures.

6. On a scale of 1-5, I would rate my belief in this claim at a 2. While the idea is intriguing, the limited language sample and lack of consideration for tonal and context-dependent languages make the conclusions seem premature and likely biased. To be more compelling, the research would need to be expanded and replicated across a far more diverse set of languages and cultures.
