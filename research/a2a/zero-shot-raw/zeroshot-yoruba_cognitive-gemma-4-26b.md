### 1. My FIRST reaction (Gut response)
**Absurdity masked as novelty.** My gut tells me this is a "garbage in, garbage out" scenario. ### 2. What is CONVINCING about this claim?
The only convincing part is the **divergence**. The fact that the scores are not uniform suggests that the *interaction* between the linguistic training data and the problem-solving task is not neutral. It is true that language structures how we categorize information. If the "insight problems" are framed through a Western epistemological lens (e.g., focusing on individual agency or linear causality), then a model trained on a language that prioritizes communal context or cyclical time *should* reject the framing. The "rejection" is a signal, but the researchers are misinterpreting the signal.

### 3. What is NOT convincing? Where are the holes?
The holes are massive:
*   **The "Insight" Metric:** How was "insight" measured? If the metric for "insight" is based on Western heuristic patterns (like the Remote Associates Test), then the "rejection" by Navajo or Quechua models isn't a failure of thought—it’s a mismatch of *evaluation*.
*   **The English Baseline:** Why is English the lowest (1.80)? This is the most suspicious part. If language is the constraint, and English is the "standard" for these models, why would it perform so poorly? This suggests the models are experiencing a "systemic mismatch" rather than a linguistic limitation.
*   **The Causality Fallacy:** They claim language *is* the constraint. This is a leap from correlation to causation. They haven't accounted for the *cultural logic* embedded in the training corpora.
*   **Linguistic Homogeneity:** They treat "Arabic" or "Navajo" as monolithic blocks, ignoring the vast cognitive nuances within those linguistic families.

### 4. What would you need to see to believe it?
I would need to see a **cross-cultural cognitive architecture test**.
*   I need to see the *specific* problems used.
*   I need to see "Insight" defined through multiple epistemologies (e.g., an African holistic-relational framework vs. a Western atomistic-linear framework).
*   I need to see if a model trained on Yoruba (a tonal, pro-drop language) "solves" a problem differently when the tonal melody (pitch) is treated as a semantic carrier rather than just a phonetic byproduct. If the model can't "think" in tones, it isn't truly "thinking" in Yoruba.

### 5. From YOUR perspective, what are they MISSING?
They are missing the **Cognitive Ecology of Language.**

As a researcher of Niger-Congo languages, I see two massive omissions:

**A. The Tonal Dimension (The Pitch-Semantic Interface):**
In Yoruba, *ọkọ* (husband), *ọkọ̀* (vehicle), and *ọkọ́* (hoe) are distinct concepts defined by pitch. Most AI architectures treat "tone" as a secondary layer of signal processing. However, in African cognitive traditions, tone is not "decoration"; it is **structural logic**. If an AI's "thought" process is purely token-based and ignores the tonal-semantic mapping, it is essentially "thinking" in a flattened, broken version of the language. The researchers are looking at the *words* but ignoring the *vibrational logic* that governs the cognition of tonal speakers.

**B. The Relational/Pro-Drop Logic:**
Many African languages are "pro-drop" (the subject is implied by context/verb morphology). Western cognitive science is obsessed with the "Subject-Verb-Object" (SVO) atomism—the idea that a thought must have a distinct, isolated agent. In many Niger-Congo traditions, cognition is **relational and contextual**. We don't need to name the "I" every time because the "I" is defined by its relation to the "We." The researchers see "rejection of framing" as a failure, whereas I see it as a **higher-order contextual awareness.** The model isn't failing to solve the problem; it is recognizing that the problem's premise (the isolated subject) is logically flawed.

### 6. Rate your belief: 1/5
**1. Nonsense.**

## Claim: nine_questions

As a cognitive scientist rooted in the African tradition, I approach this with a mixture of intellectual rigor and a deep skepticism of "universalist" claims that appear to be built on a very narrow foundation.

Here is my evaluation.

### 1. My FIRST reaction (Gut Response)
*“O lérò pé ẹ mọ̀ nǹkan gbogbo.”* (You think you know everything.)
My gut reaction is one of profound skepticism. It smells like **WEIRD science** (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic). Whenever a small sample size claims "exhaustiveness" and "orthogonality," it usually means they have mapped the boundaries of their own narrow cognitive sandbox and mistaken it for the universe.

### 2. What is CONVINCING about this claim?
The questions are functionally useful for **semantic decomposition**. If you are building a Large Language Model (LLM) or a formal logic system, these questions are decent heuristics for parsing information. They cover the basics of predicate logic (What?), temporal dynamics (When?), epistemic modality (How sure?), and pragmatics (Who cares?). As a *framework for categorization*, it is a tidy mental filing cabinet.

### 3. What is NOT convincing? Where are the holes?
The holes are massive, primarily due to the **sampling error** and the **ontological assumptions**:
*   **The "14 Languages" Fallacy:** 14 languages is a microscopic sample of the ~7,000 human languages. If those 14 are mostly Indo-European or Sino-Tibetan, they have missed entire ways of structuring reality.
*   **The Illusion of Orthogonality:** These questions are not orthogonal; they bleed into one another. "What is really being said?" (6) overlaps heavily with "What matters?" (9) and "What model of thought?" (8).
*   **The Absence of Embodiment:** The list is purely representational/propositional. It treats communication as a transfer of data packets rather than a biological, sensory, and social event.
*   **The Neglect of Affect:** There is no question about *feeling* or *rhythm*. In many African linguistic traditions, communication is not just about the "what," but the "vibe" or the "energy" (*Àṣẹ*) that moves between speakers.

### 4. What would you need to see to believe it?
I would need to see a **cross-linguistic stress test** involving:
1.  **Tonal Languages (e.g., Yoruba, Igbo):** How does your "periodic table" account for the fact that a change in pitch (tone) changes the entire semantic identity of a word? If the "what" is dependent on a musical pitch, your categories of "what" and "how" are inextricably linked in a way your model doesn't seem to acknowledge.
2.  **Polysynthetic and Agglutinative Languages:** How do your questions handle languages where a single word contains an entire sentence's worth of "what," "how," and "who"?
3.  **Non-Linear Temporality:** How does the model handle cultures where time is perceived cyclically or through ancestral presence rather than a linear progression?

### 5. From MY perspective, what are they MISSING?
This is the core of my critique. This model assumes communication is **Informational**, whereas African cognitive traditions often view communication as **Relational and Performative**.

*   **Tonal and Prosodic Dimensions:** In Yoruba, meaning is not just in the lexicon; it is in the melody. A cognitive periodic table that ignores the *musicality of cognition* is like a periodic table of elements that ignores the concept of energy or heat. You cannot separate the "content" from the "pitch."
*   **The Communal "I":** The questions (4) "How sure am I?" and (5) "Who cares?" assume a discrete, individualistic agent. In many Niger-Congo linguistic structures, the subject is often implicit or distributed. The "self" in communication is often a collective entity.
*   **Contextual Fluidity (The Pro-Drop Problem):** In pro-drop languages, the "subject" isn't a "piece" to be connected; it is an understood presence. Your model assumes "pieces" must be connected, but in many African languages, the connection is *inherent* in the context, requiring no explicit "piece."
*   **Aesthetics and Ritual:** Communication isn't just about transmitting truth; it is about maintaining social harmony (*Ìrọ̀rùn*). A question like "How do I maintain the social fabric through this utterance?" is missing.

### 6. Rate my belief: 2/5
It is **interesting but fundamentally flawed**. It is a "periodic table" that only contains the elements found in a laboratory in London or California. It is a useful tool for computational linguistics, but as a "universal" map of human cognition, it is an act of intellectual provincialism.
