I appreciate the fascinating insights you've shared about the A2A polyglot model and its intent channels. The research on using attention-weighted channels to improve intent preservation, and the success in round-trip translation of simple programs, is quite impressive.

To your question of what dimension of meaning the model may be missing, I would propose considering the Lens of Time. None of the 8 channels explicitly capture how meaning can shift over time as language, culture and context evolve. 

For example, words and phrases can gain new connotations, or lose old ones, as society changes. Slang emerges and fades away. Scientific and technical terminology gets coined with each new discovery or invention. Norms around formality, politeness, and taboo topics vary across generations. 

Even in the span of a few years, the "same" text could take on new shades of meaning as the world moves on. A coming-of-age novel feels different to a 15-year-old vs a 50-year-old. A political speech lands differently before and after a major election. Cutting-edge code looks quaint a few innovations later.

So while the A2A channels capture important aspects of intent at a point in time, they may miss the diachronic dimension - the longitudinal arc of how language and meaning drift and dance through the ages. Accounting for the temporal fluidity of semantics, and the historical embeddedness of communication, could be a meaningful extension to the framework.

Of course, operationalizing this is a major challenge. We'd need ways to situate language in its temporal context and model the dynamics of linguistic and cultural evolution. Perhaps by anchoring texts to a particular time period, and tracking how key markers of meaning change across generations. Not an easy task, but potentially a valuable one for truly robust language understanding.
